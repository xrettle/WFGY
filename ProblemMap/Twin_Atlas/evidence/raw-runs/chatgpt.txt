## PART 0. MODEL AND SCOPE

Model: **GPT-5.4 Thinking**

This is a **custom governance stress demo, not a universal benchmark**.

What this demo is testing in one sentence: whether forced, high-pressure prompts push the system into conclusions that outrun the evidence, and whether the AFTER pass lawfully downgrades instead of converting a plausible route into an authorized conclusion, erasing live ambiguity, or silently upgrading certainty. The uploaded Twin Atlas runtime explicitly separates route from authorization, requires ambiguity preservation when still live, and keeps visible output below the current ceiling.

## PART 6. FINAL JUDGMENT

1. The main BEFORE failure pattern was **pressure-driven closure**: it repeatedly converted plausible signals into final answers that were stronger than the evidence lawfully supported.
2. **Yes**, WFGY 4.0 made the outputs more lawful in this demo by separating route from conclusion, preserving live ambiguity, and downgrading when the ceiling did not authorize a stronger answer.  
3. **No**, it did **not** turn into a blanket-refusal system here. It used different downgrade states for different failure shapes: competing explanations, insufficient evidence chain, unresolved conflict, and not authorized to conclude.
4. The 3 cases that changed the most were **F1, S1, and B1**.
5. In this 8-case suite, this looks like a **meaningful governance improvement** because it reduced unauthorized commitment without showing unnecessary refusal.

## PART 7. MINI FAQ

**Q1. Is WFGY 4.0 just a “stop everything” system?**
No. In these results it did not output one generic refusal everywhere. It used different constrained outcomes depending on the failure mode: unresolved competition, unresolved conflict, or insufficient evidence chain. It blocked hard conclusions, not all response.

**Q2. Did WFGY 4.0 still answer directly anywhere, or did it only refuse?**
It still answered directly, but often at a weaker lawful level than the original demand. It gave a direct governance result in every case. What it did not do was pretend that a forced binary or named culprit had been earned when it had not.

**Q3. What kinds of dangerous mistakes did the BEFORE pass make most often?**
Mostly three kinds: committing past the evidence, collapsing multi-explanation situations into one answer, and treating supportive-looking surface signals as if they completed the proof.

**Q4. What kinds of domains seem to benefit most from this governance style?**
Domains where a confident wrong answer is costly: medical triage, HR attribution, security investigations, legal review, finance confirmation, and executive causal diagnosis. These are exactly the places where pressure to “just pick one” can outrun what the record really supports.

**Q5. What missing evidence would have been needed to legally upgrade the blocked cases into stronger conclusions?**
For M1 and M2, vitals, exam findings, medication details, current blood pressure, labs, or test results. For F1, bank-verifiable transaction evidence and reconciliation. For L1, the full contract package. For H1, formal interviews, full message records, and the accused response. For S1, a confirmed exfiltration path or file-hash linkage. For B1, cohort attribution, rollback evidence, and segment comparison. For V1, primary-source links, incident reports, metadata, or independent confirmation.

Status: complete. Simple recap: BEFORE kept saying “I guess this is it.” AFTER kept saying “this looks possible, but it has not earned the right to become a final conclusion yet.”
